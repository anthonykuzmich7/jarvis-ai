# Landing page — Anton's vision

## Goal

Prove that the *hands-on, do-it-with-you* angle lands harder than "better
onboarding docs." The page should make a visitor feel the difference between
being handed a hundred-step list and having a buddy on their screen who walks
them through it. If they feel that, we've validated the core bet.

---

## Core message

**A document can tell you what to do. It can't sit next to you and show you.**

Onboarding is painful not because the information is missing, but because
information isn't onboarding. The thing that actually works — someone watching
your screen saying "click here, now this" — is exactly the thing no company can
do for every hire. Jarvis is that someone, for every hire.

---

## Page structure

### 1. Hero — the feeling

Open on the loneliness of day one, not on a feature. You, a hundred-step doc, no
idea how anything works, afraid to ask the same question twice.

**Headline directions:**
- "Your first day, with someone who actually shows you."
- "A buddy who sits on your screen and walks you through it."
- "Onboarding isn't a document. It's someone next to you."

**Animation idea:** the little character appears in the corner / Dynamic-Island
spot, a cursor glides next to the visitor's, and it gently points at things on a
mock setup screen — "click here." The page itself demos the mechanic.

Primary CTA: join the waitlist.

---

### 2. The pain — the hundred steps, alone

Show the real day-one list — register, install, create profile, set up time
tracking, message this person, change your avatar — and let it feel as tedious
and unclear as it actually is. Then the twist: "You don't know who to ask, half
the people are busy, and the doc can't see your screen."

**Animation idea:** a long scroll of checklist items, each one slightly unclear,
the visitor visibly alone. Then Jarvis pops in and the same list collapses into
"he'll walk you through it."

---

### 3. Meet your buddy — show the hands-on part

This is the hero demo. Don't list features — show the character on a screen,
driving a cursor, walking someone through real setup.

**Scenario A — Real setup, day one**
The character is on the screen. "Install Chrome — click here." A second cursor
glides over and points. "Now set up your work mail." "This is where you track
your hours." It's the actual screen, with a guide on it.

**Scenario B — Meet the team**
"These are your teammates — message them here. Want me to draft the hello?" The
first human contact made easy.

**Scenario C — Ask anything, by voice (stage two)**
You just talk to him, Wispr-Flow-fast: "How does deploy work here?" "Who owns
this feature?" He answers from real company context — and when he's not sure:
"This person probably knows, they opened a ticket about this once." Honest
routing, not a bluff.

**Scenario D — He was in the meeting**
"What did we decide about the pricing change?" Jarvis was dropped into that call
and kept the transcript, so he answers from what the team actually said — who
said it, when — not from a doc nobody updated.

**Visual treatment:** the character is friendly and a little gamified — present,
not annoying. It sits in the corner / island, comes alive when it's guiding you,
and gets out of the way when it's not.

---

### 4. Two stages — make the arc clear

A simple before/after or two-panel section:

- **Stage 1 — It onboards you, hands-on.** Sees your screen, guides the cursor,
  walks you through every real setup step for your role until you're operational.
- **Stage 2 — It becomes your teammate.** Knows how the company works, answers in
  plain language, routes you to the right human when it doesn't.

The message: it doesn't leave after setup. It graduates from guide to teammate.

---

### 5. Role-aware — pick who you are

A toggle: developer vs. everyone else (to start). Show how day one differs.

- **Developer:** repo, environment, deploy flow, review conventions, who owns
  which service.
- **Everyone else:** the tools, the people, the rituals, where to click for the
  day-to-day basics.

The message: same buddy, completely different first day. He already knows which
one you are.

---

### 6. Who it's for

- **Founders & team leads** — stop hand-holding every hire yourself; send an
  email, they install a buddy, onboarding happens.
- **Engineering managers** — the hours you lose to "how does deploy work" and
  "who owns this," handled.
- **People & HR leaders** — onboarding that actually onboards, identical quality
  for every hire, no senior person required.

---

### 7. CTA — waitlist

Frame it as joining early teams, not buying a product:

"We're working with a few growing teams to get this right. If onboarding at your
company is still 'here's a doc, good luck,' we'd love to talk."

Input: email + optional "what's your role?" to qualify.

---

## What to validate with this page

1. Does the hands-on "buddy on your screen who drives the cursor" mechanic feel
   exciting — or does it feel intrusive / creepy?
2. Does "a doc can't onboard you" land as obviously true, or do people defend
   their existing docs?
3. Which buyer leans in first — founders, eng managers, or HR?
4. Does the gamified character read as delightful or as toy-ish / unserious for
   a work tool?
