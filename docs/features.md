# Features

Jarvis has four core jobs. The first three are what he *does*; the fourth is the
posture — he's a teammate, not a tool.

## 1. Onboarding and self-serve access

When a new person joins, Jarvis provides the accesses they need — **without an IT
specialist in the loop**. He grants the access himself.

He also knows the company's processes and can answer onboarding questions
directly, for example:

- how merge requests are created here,
- how to deploy a new service,
- who to reach out to for what (e.g. "do I need DevOps to create this, or not?"),
- when certain parts of the project were built and how they fit together.

The goal: a new hire is productive on day one or two instead of week two, and the
IT team stops being a bottleneck.

## 2. Company knowledge and context

Jarvis is connected to the company's data and knowledge sources — for example
**Confluence** — so he understands how the company actually works.

He holds context about:

- **the company itself**: what the product is, how it works, how the company
  makes money,
- **people**: who does what, so he can point you to the right person,
- **the code / main products**: enough to advise you.

Concrete example: you're building something and get stuck. Jarvis can tell you
"go ask X, they built this part." He behaves like the real teammate you'd lean on
in your first months — the one who manages your accesses *and* answers your
questions.

## 3. Your personal work context

Jarvis carries **your** context, not just the company's. He has access to the
things that inform your work:

- your **Slack conversations** and their context,
- your **meetings** — Google Meet, Zoom — and what was discussed,
- your **contacts** — the people you work with.

Why this matters: today, when you start a new feature, you hunt through Slack,
docs, and past meetings to gather context and paste it into a chat. Jarvis
already has it. You connect your AI tool to him (e.g. **Claude Code over MCP**),
and it starts already knowing what you're working on — it checks whether the
context exists before you have to go find it.

## 4. An ongoing teammate

Put together, Jarvis is pitched as an **onboarding body** that then sticks around
as a coworker. He doesn't just get you in the door — he keeps helping you do the
work.

The bet: people will genuinely *want* this in their company. It saves IT
specialists the time they spend managing accesses, and it removes the painful,
slow, who-do-I-even-ask part of starting somewhere new.
