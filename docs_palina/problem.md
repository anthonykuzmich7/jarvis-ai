# The problem (Palina)

## Onboarding is slow because it's not personalized

When you join a company, you get a generic onboarding pack. It's the same doc
the last ten people received, regardless of whether they're a backend engineer,
a product designer, or a business analyst. The information exists, but it's not
filtered for you — so you spend your first days figuring out what's relevant and
what isn't.

The result: instead of getting productive, you're doing archaeology.

## What actually happens in week one

A developer joins. Day one: they get GitHub access (maybe), a link to the wiki,
and a 1:1 with their manager scheduled for Thursday. By day three, they still
don't know how to run the local environment because the README is three versions
out of date. They've sent four Slack messages to people they don't know yet.

A designer joins the same company on the same day. They're added to 47 Slack
channels and have no idea which ones matter. Their first Figma file is a
200-frame document with no explanation of what's live and what's exploration.
They schedule a 1:1 to figure out who the PM is.

A PM joins. They have five 1:1s in the first week and still don't understand
what the team shipped last quarter or why.

These aren't bad companies. This is just what unassisted onboarding looks like
when three people with completely different needs are handed the same playbook.

## The blockers that compound

Week one isn't slow because people aren't trying. It's slow because blockers
pile up and there's no one to route around them:

- **Access you don't have yet.** You find out you need a tool when you open it,
  not when you start. The request goes to someone who takes three days to respond.
- **Questions you don't know to ask.** You don't know the right vocabulary yet,
  so you can't search Confluence effectively. You're searching for answers you
  can't name.
- **People who aren't available.** Your buddy is in a different timezone. The
  person who owns the system you need is on vacation. You wait.
- **Information that exists but isn't yours.** It's in Slack threads from eight
  months ago, in a Confluence page that was last updated when the product looked
  different, in someone's head. Technically accessible; practically invisible.

Each of these is a half-day lost. In two weeks, that's a week of real time.

## Why generic onboarding fails different roles

A developer joining a company needs: how to set up the environment (their
specific environment, not the default one), how deploy pipelines work, who owns
the services they'll touch, what the PR process looks like here.

A designer needs: where the design system lives and when it was last updated,
how design reviews work at this company, who the PM is and what's in the current
sprint, which Figma files are live versus exploration.

A PM needs: current OKRs and the reasoning behind them, how reporting works,
which data sources exist, what decisions are pending that need new-hire context.

None of these is the other. Onboarding gives all three people the same thing and
asks them to self-sort. This is friction that compounds in the first two weeks.

## The knowledge that exists but isn't contextual

Companies have the information. It's in Confluence, in Notion, in Slack history,
in Google Drive, in people's heads. The problem isn't that it doesn't exist —
it's that it's not:

- **findable** without knowing what to look for,
- **curated** for your specific role,
- **current** (most docs are out of date within months),
- **contextual** — available at the moment you actually need it, inside the tool
  you're using, not buried in a folder you have to remember to open.

The answer isn't a better knowledge base. Nobody opens the knowledge base when
they're confused. They open Slack and hope someone is online.

## The longer-term decay problem

Even after onboarding ends, the problem continues. Company knowledge decays
constantly: roadmaps shift, people leave, products change. The new hire who was
just onboarded is now supposed to onboard the next person — but their knowledge
is already weeks old, and nobody updated the docs.

Every generation of employees gets a slightly worse version of the company's
actual knowledge.

## Who pays

- **New hires** spend one to two weeks in partial confusion instead of building.
- **Managers and senior employees** absorb the 1:1 load — answering the same
  questions for every new person, every time.
- **HR teams** maintain generic docs that nobody fully reads.
- **The company** loses productivity it already paid for by hiring someone.
