# The problem (Palina)

## Onboarding is slow because it's not personalized

When you join a company, you get a generic onboarding pack. It's the same doc
the last ten people received, regardless of whether they're a backend engineer,
a product designer, or a business analyst. The information exists, but it's not
filtered for you, so you spend your first days figuring out what's relevant and
what isn't.

The result: instead of getting productive, you're doing archaeology.

## What actually happens in week one

- **You get a long HR document** that covers everything for everyone. Most of it
  doesn't apply to your role. You read it anyway, just in case.
- **You schedule 1:1s** to gather the context that isn't in any doc — who owns
  what, what the team is actually working on right now, what the unwritten rules
  are. These meetings take days to get and hours to process.
- **You get added to every Slack channel** and have no idea which ones matter
  for your work. You either ignore everything or read too much.
- **You don't know the product well enough** to ask the right questions. You
  lack context to understand the answers you're getting.

## Why generic onboarding fails different roles

A developer joining a company needs to know: how to set up the local
environment, how deploy pipelines work, who owns the services they'll touch,
what the code review process looks like.

A designer needs to know: where the design system lives, how design reviews
work, who the PM is, what the research says about current user problems.

A business analyst needs to know: how reporting works, what data sources exist,
who the stakeholders are, what decisions are pending.

None of these is the other. Today, onboarding gives all three people the same
thing and asks them to self-sort. This is friction that compounds daily in the
first weeks.

## The knowledge that exists but isn't accessible

Companies have the information. It's in Confluence, in Notion, in Slack history,
in Google Drive, in people's heads. The problem isn't that it doesn't exist —
it's that it's not:

- **findable** without knowing what to look for,
- **curated** for your specific role,
- **current** (most docs are out of date within months),
- **contextual** — available at the moment you actually need it, not buried in a
  folder you'll forget about.

## The longer-term decay problem

Even after onboarding ends, the problem continues. Company knowledge decays
constantly: roadmaps shift, people leave, products change. The new hire who was
just onboarded is now supposed to onboard the next person — but their knowledge
is already weeks old, and nobody has updated the docs.

The result is a game of telephone. Every generation of employees gets a slightly
worse version of the company's actual knowledge.

## Who pays

- **New hires** spend one to two weeks in partial confusion instead of building
  something useful.
- **Managers and senior employees** absorb the 1:1 load — answering the same
  questions for every new person, repeatedly.
- **HR teams** maintain generic docs that nobody fully reads.
- **The company** loses productivity it already paid for by hiring someone.
