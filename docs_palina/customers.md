# Customers and positioning (Palina)

## Who feels this pain most

The onboarding problem exists in every company that hires people. But the pain
is sharpest in companies where:

- roles are specialized and distinct (developers, designers, analysts, sales all
  have different first-week needs),
- the team is growing fast enough that senior people can't personally onboard
  everyone,
- knowledge is scattered across multiple tools (Confluence, Notion, Slack,
  Google Drive, Jira).

That points to **tech companies and scale-ups** as the primary market — teams
that are too big for informal onboarding but not yet big enough to have a
dedicated onboarding team. 50–500 people, growing. Engineering- or
product-led. They've tried Confluence; it's a mess.

## Buyers, in priority order

1. **People / HR leadership** — they own the onboarding experience and feel the
   failure directly. When new hires are slow to ramp, it's HR's problem.
   Jarvis gives them something measurable: personalized, role-aware onboarding
   that doesn't depend on a senior engineer having time. They can run it per
   hire, per role, per department.

2. **Engineering managers** — every new developer costs them four to six hours
   in the first week: environment help, context dumps, same questions repeated.
   Jarvis handles those. The manager shows up to the first 1:1 talking about
   actual work, not Confluence navigation.

3. **Design and product leadership** — designers and PMs have their own
   onboarding gaps (design systems, research context, product strategy) that
   generic HR docs don't address. A head of design who's watched three new
   designers spend two weeks confused about Figma structure immediately
   understands the pitch.

4. **IT / DevOps** — access requests that could have been predicted on day one
   stop being surprises. Jarvis surfaces what each role typically needs before
   the employee hits the wall.

## What we're selling

Not a knowledge base. Not a chatbot. Not another HR tool.

We're selling **the first week back** — the productivity that new hires lose
while figuring out where everything is. And we're selling it to the people who
are currently paying for that loss without a clear way to fix it.

The pitch to HR: "Your new hires are spending their first week in 1:1s and
reading docs that don't apply to them. Jarvis gives each person a personalized
guide on day one — different for a developer than for a designer."

The pitch to engineering managers: "Every time someone new joins your team,
you spend four to six hours that week answering the same questions. Jarvis
handles those."

The pitch to design leads: "Your new designer spent their first week confused
about which Figma files are live. That doesn't show up in any HR metric, but
you know it happened. Jarvis doesn't let it happen."

## How we're different

**Not a knowledge base you have to visit.** Jarvis appears on top of the tools
the employee is already using — Slack, Figma, their IDE, any app — via a
keyboard shortcut or voice. The information comes to them, in the moment they
need it, inside the context where the question arose.

**Not a reactive search.** Jarvis is proactive. He monitors the company's
context sources (Slack, calendar, Jira) and surfaces what matters before you
ask — similar to how Victor.ai works for executives, applied to every new hire
and every employee. He does context work in the background so your morning
starts with clarity, not catch-up.

**Not generic.** A developer and a designer join the same company on the same
day. They get completely different day-one experiences from Jarvis. The
filtering and sequencing is the product.

**Not a blocker source.** When something blocks a new hire — a vacation,
a missing access, a question nobody's answered — Jarvis routes around it.
He surfaces who else can help, drafts the message, makes sure the person
keeps moving.

**Not a one-time tool.** After onboarding ends, Jarvis becomes the company's
living memory — updated continuously, accessible to anyone. Month one or
year three, you can ask "how does X work here?" and get a current answer.

## The comparison set

Buyers may compare Jarvis to:
- **Confluence / Notion** — static docs, you have to find them, they go stale.
  Jarvis surfaces the right piece in context and keeps it current.
- **Generic AI chatbots** (ChatGPT, etc.) — not connected to the company's
  actual context. Good for general knowledge, not for "who owns this service."
- **HR onboarding tools** (Workday, BambooHR flows) — checklist-based, not
  personalized, not aware of the actual work environment.
- **Internal wikis** — same problem as Confluence.

None of these solve for context, personalization, or proactivity simultaneously.
That's the gap.
