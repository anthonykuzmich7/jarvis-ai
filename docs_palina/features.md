# Features (Palina)

Jarvis has five things that make it different from a knowledge base or a
chatbot. Together they create something closer to a personal guide than a tool.

## 1. Always-available overlay — keyboard shortcut and voice

Jarvis lives on your desktop, not in a browser tab you have to remember to open.
You summon him with a keyboard shortcut or by speaking — similar to how Wispr
Flow works for voice input. He appears on top of whatever you're doing.

This matters because onboarding questions don't happen when you're sitting in a
knowledge base. They happen when you're already inside Slack, or trying to open
a tool for the first time, or looking at a codebase you don't understand yet.
Jarvis meets you there.

## 2. Role-aware personalized onboarding

When you join, Jarvis knows your role — designer, developer, business analyst,
sales — and builds your onboarding around it.

He doesn't surface the master HR document. He surfaces the five things a
designer specifically needs to know on day one. Then the next five things on day
two. The information is the same as it's always been; the filtering is new.

Concrete examples of what role-aware onboarding looks like:
- A developer gets: environment setup, deploy pipeline, code review process, who
  owns which services.
- A designer gets: design system location, how design reviews work, current
  research findings, the PM they'll work with most.
- A business analyst gets: what data sources exist, reporting tools, pending
  decisions they'll need context on.

## 3. Contextual app overlay — guidance inside the tools you're using

When you open Slack for the first time, Jarvis appears on top of it. He doesn't
make you go somewhere else to learn about Slack — he walks you through it in
place:

- "This is your manager, Maya. Want me to help you draft a hello message?"
- "These are the three channels your team actually uses — the rest you can
  ignore for now."
- "You've been added to #all-engineering, but for your first sprint the
  relevant channel is #team-growth."

Same mechanic in other tools: open your company's project management tool and
Jarvis explains what the current sprint is about, who owns what, and what's
blocking what — without you having to ask.

The experience is interactive and a little playful. It's not a tooltip. It's a
conversation.

## 4. Mascot and gamification

Jarvis has a character — a small mascot — that makes the onboarding feel like
something you're doing with someone, not something being done to you.

There's a light gamification layer: progress through onboarding stages, small
milestones when you've connected with your manager, set up your tools, read the
first roadmap update. This isn't points for the sake of points — it's a signal
that you're actually moving forward, which is what new hires lack most in their
first two weeks.

The mascot also creates continuity. When Jarvis reappears three months later to
tell you about a product update, you recognize him. He's not a notification.
He's a familiar presence.

## 5. Living company knowledge base

After onboarding, Jarvis stays. He becomes the place where company knowledge
lives and gets updated.

- Roadmaps get added as they change.
- Decisions get logged with context.
- Processes get updated when they evolve.

Any employee — new or old — can ask Jarvis a question about the company and get
a current answer, not one from a doc written eighteen months ago.

This is the shareable knowledge memory: the company's institutional knowledge,
always accessible, always up to date, and searchable by anyone who needs it.
