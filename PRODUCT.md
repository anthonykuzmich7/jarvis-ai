# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

IT managers, HR / People leadership (CPOs, HR partners), and engineering managers at companies with 20–500 people. They are watching new hires sit unproductive for two weeks waiting on access, while IT specialists drown in repetitive provisioning tickets. They know the problem is expensive; they just haven't found a solution that doesn't require a full identity-management platform.

## Product Purpose

Jarvis is an AI teammate companies "hire" to handle new-hire onboarding: granting accesses, answering the "who do I even ask?" questions, and carrying each employee's personal work context across tools. It lives in Slack, acts as a real user in company systems, and exposes context to AI tools over MCP. The landing page exists to validate that this pain is real and worth paying to solve — the primary success signal is waitlist signups from the right people.

## Brand Personality

Friendly, fast, trustworthy. Jarvis is the brilliant colleague who just happens to know everything about the company — warm and accessible, not robotic or corporate. The visual language is the Geniestudio reference: playful pastel sky, airy whitespace, rounded everything, Pressed Charcoal CTAs against a pale blue canvas.

## Anti-references

- Hype AI startup (glowing purple-to-blue gradients, animated orbs, "next-gen AI" language, screaming hero metrics)
- Generic dark SaaS clones (Vercel/Linear aesthetic applied without intent — the current site leans this way)
- Corporate IT enterprise (ServiceNow-style navy blue, dense tables, zero personality)

## Design Principles

1. **The colleague, not the chatbot.** Every design decision should reinforce that Jarvis is a person you work *with*, not a tool you configure. Warmth over precision.
2. **Approachable confidence.** The product solves a real IT pain — it can be authoritative without being cold. The Geniestudio palette + copy voice achieves this balance.
3. **Problem first, product second.** Visitors should feel "yes, that's us" before they see a feature. Lead with the pain, not the demo.
4. **One decisive ask.** Every section funnels toward the waitlist. No competing CTAs, no confusion about what to do next.
5. **Airy, not empty.** Generous whitespace is a feature, not a cop-out. Use it deliberately; contrast it with dense, warm card moments.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum. Reduced-motion already handled at the CSS level (existing `@media (prefers-reduced-motion)` in globals.css). Ensure all form labels are associated, focus states are visible, and color is never the sole carrier of meaning.
